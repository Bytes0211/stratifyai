Here’s a concise but comprehensive description of evolution — what it is, how it works, evidence for it, common patterns, and frequent misconceptions.

What evolution is
- Evolution is the change in the genetic composition of populations over successive generations. It explains how the diversity of life arises and how organisms become better matched (or simply different) to their environments over time.
- It operates at the level of populations, not individual organisms.

Primary mechanisms
- Natural selection: Individuals with heritable traits that increase their survival or reproductive success tend to leave more offspring, so those traits become more common. Selection acts on phenotypes but changes genotypes over time.
- Mutation: Random changes in DNA that introduce new genetic variation. Most are neutral or harmful; a few can be beneficial.
- Genetic drift: Random fluctuations in allele frequencies, especially important in small populations (founder effects, bottlenecks).
- Gene flow (migration): Movement of genes between populations through interbreeding; it can introduce new alleles or homogenize populations.
- Sexual selection: A form of selection based on mating success (e.g., bright plumage, courtship behaviors), which can favor traits that increase reproductive success even if they have survival costs.

Outcomes and patterns
- Adaptation: Traits that increase fitness in a given environment. Adaptations arise by selection acting on variation.
- Speciation: The splitting of one lineage into two or more reproductively isolated lineages. Types include allopatric (geographic separation), sympatric (within the same area), and others.
- Convergent evolution: Unrelated organisms evolve similar traits when adapting to similar environments (e.g., wings in bats and birds).
- Coevolution: Reciprocal evolutionary change between interacting species (e.g., pollinators and flowers).
- Adaptive radiation: Rapid diversification into many forms, often following colonization of new environments (Darwin’s finches on the Galápagos).
- Punctuated equilibrium vs gradualism: Patterns of change through time — sometimes slow and gradual, sometimes long stasis punctuated by rapid change.

Evidence for evolution
- Fossils: Transitional forms (e.g., Tiktaalik bridging fish and tetrapods) and sequences of changes in the fossil record.
- Comparative anatomy: Homologous structures (same basic anatomy in different species) indicate common ancestry; vestigial organs show reduced past functions.
- Embryology: Similar early developmental stages in related organisms.
- Biogeography: Geographic distributions reflect evolutionary history (island endemism, continental patterns).
- Molecular biology: DNA and protein sequences reveal relationships and show shared ancestry; molecular clocks estimate divergence times.
- Direct observation: Rapid evolution seen in bacteria (antibiotic resistance), viruses, insects (pesticide resistance), and laboratory experiments.

Modern understanding
- The modern synthesis combined Darwinian selection with Mendelian genetics. Since then, fields like population genetics, evo-devo (evolutionary developmental biology), genomics, and the neutral theory have refined and extended evolutionary theory.
- Not all evolutionary change is adaptive; much molecular variation is neutral and shaped by drift.

Common misconceptions
- Evolution is not goal-directed or progressive — it does not strive toward “perfection.”
- Individuals do not evolve; populations do.
- “Survival of the fittest” does not always mean the strongest; fitness means reproductive success in a given environment.
- Evolution does not imply improvement in every context; it simply changes frequencies of heritable traits.

Everyday examples
- Antibiotic resistance in bacteria.
- Pesticide resistance in insects.
- Changes in moth coloration during the Industrial Revolution (classic example).
- Lactose tolerance in human populations associated with dairy farming.
